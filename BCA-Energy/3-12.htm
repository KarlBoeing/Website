BCA Volume 2 - Energy Efficiency Measures
for Residential Buildings - Class 1

The energy efficiency measures for houses were introduced in 2003 in Volume 2 of the BCA. Those measures have been subsequently adopted by all States and Territories.

The Objective is to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by efficiently using energy.

The aim of the energy efficiency provision is also to encourage superior design and construction methods by eliminating "worst practice".

Part 2.6 BCA PERFORMANCE REQUIREMENTS

P2.6.1 Building
A building must have, to the degree necessary, a level of thermal performance to facilitate the efficient use of energy for artificial heating and cooling
P2.6.2 Services
A building's domestic services, including any associated distribution system and components must have features that, to the degree necessary, facilitate the efficient use of energy

    The following definitions are used in Part 3.12
    Conditioned space means a space within a building that is heated or cooled by the building's domestic services, excluding a non-habitable room in which a heater with a capacity of not more than 1.2 kW is installed.
    Piping means an assembly of pipes, with or without valves or other fittings, connected together for the conveyance of liquids.
    Reflective insulation means a building membrane with a reflective surface such as a reflective foil laminate, reflective barrier, foil batt or the like capable of reducing radiant heat flow.
    R-Value means the thermal resistance (m2.K/W) of a component calculated by dividing its thickness by its thermal conductivity.
    Total R-Value means the sum of the R-Values of the individual component layers in a composite element including the air space and associated surface resistances.
    Total U-Value means the thermal transmittance (W/m2.K) of the composite element including the air space and associated surface transmittance.
    Ventilation opening means an opening in the external wall, floor or roof of a building designed to allow air movement into or out of the building by natural means including a permanent opening, an openable part of a window, a door or other device which can be held open.

    Climate zone, for the purposes of Part 2.6 and Part 3.12, means an area defined in Table 1.1.2 for specific locations, having energy efficiency provisions based on a range of similar climatic characteristics.
    Western Australia has five climate zones designated:
    Zone 1 - High humidity summer, warm winter
    Zone 3 - Hot dry summer, warm winter
    Zone 4 - Hot dry summer, cool winter
    Zone 5 - Warm Temperate
    Zone 6 - Mild Temperate

    The Energy Provisions reflects the ABCB objective of delivering minimum cost-effective measures.

    Building fabric or building envelope is a term used to describe the roof, walls, windows, floors and internal walls of a home. The envelope controls heat gain in summer and heat loss in winter.
    Building fabric separating heated or cooled interior spaces from the exterior of the building or unconditioned spaces i.e. spaces that:

  • Are habitable rooms
  • Are conditioned
  • Can be conditioned

    The envelope may comprise internal or external elements. Building regulations deal with design standards for fabric heat loss, and have set minimum insulation levels in terms of elemental U values. Each element of the building envelope (roof, wall, floor, window, door) is assigned a maximum heat loss rate. The unit of measurement, Watts per square metre Kelvin (W/m²K), is an expression of  how quickly energy passes through a square metre of the element for a given temperature drop
    between inside and out (the Kelvin scale is quoted , but the temperature difference is practically measured in degrees Celsius). The aim is to slow down the rate of heat loss, which means a lower U value.

    Building fabric requirements apply to a Class 1 building, and a Class 10a building with a conditioned space.

    The glazing area limits givee in the above Table provide only minimal protection against overheating in hot conditions and heat loss during cold conditions. Careful attention to the sizing and placement of glazing around building, followeng design guidlines from reputable sources, will considerably improve both energy efficiency and comfort.

    Example 1:
    A house in climate zone 1 has three quarters of its effective glazing area consisting of single clear glass and one quarter consisting of 5 mm toned glass (all glazing has UPVC frames and is shaded by 300 mm eaves). The maximum effective glazing area is therefore three quarters of 23% plus one quarter of 30% (which equals 24.75%).
    Example 2:
    A house in climate zone 5 has two thirds of its effective glazing area shaded by 600 mm eaves and one third unshaded (all glazing has single clear glass and timber frames). The maximum effective glazing area is therefore two thirds of 40% plus one third of 28% (which equals 36%).
    Example 3:
    A house in climate zone 3 has three quarters of its effective glazing area consisting of 5 mm toned glass shaded by 450 mm eaves and one quarter consisting of single clear glass shaded by a 900 mm pergola (all glazing has aluminium standard frames). The maximum effective glazing area is therefore three quarters of 243% plus one quarter of 23% (which equals 23.75%).

    Generally if shading is used to achieve a greater % of window glazing area it must be a permanent feature such as a balcony, eaves, shading hood etc. and be no less than the required projection distance from the face of the building.
    Shading can be achieved by a shading projection (refer to Figure 3.12.2.2), a shading device or a combination of both (refer to 3.12.2.2). For example - eaves on two sides of a building and adjustable shading devices on the other two sides. A shading device satisfies any shading projection
