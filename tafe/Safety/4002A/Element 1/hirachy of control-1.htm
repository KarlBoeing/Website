Hierarchy of Control

The ‘hierarchy of control’ is a useful tool, as the order tells you which of the types of control measure provides a better level of Risk Control.

SIX WAYS TO REDUCE RISK

Start at the top and work down the list

What are you trying to do?

Examples

1) ELIMINATION

Eliminate the hazard

This is the most effective way to make the workplace safer. Always try to get rid of the hazard completely.

A kitchen hand spent many hours operating machines to slice onions and chip potatoes. This involved moving sacks of vegetables out of the store and lifting them into feed hoppers. The manual handling activity was eliminated when the owner of the kitchen decided to purchase onions and chips that were already sliced by a specialist company with mechanical equipment for lifting the large quantities that were processed.

2) SUBSTITUTION

User a safer alternative

If you cannot eliminate the hazard, replace the machinery, substances or work processes with something less hazardous.

In a restaurant kitchen, where a pedestal fan was likely to be knocked over, the owner was able to reduce the risk of injury by replacing the pedestal fan with a ceiling fan.

3) ISOLATION

Isolating or replacing a hazard

You can’t find any means of substitute the hazard then the next step would be to isolate the hazard

people working on a building site or people in the general work areas from the hazard. This can be done by installing screens or barriers or marking off hazardous areas;

4) ENGINEERING         CONTROL

Make a structural change to the work environment

If a hazard cannot be isolated, an engineering control is the next preferred measure

Let’s assume you can’t isolate the hazard an engineering control is the next preferred measure. This may include modifications to tools or equipment, providing guarding to machinery or equipment;

5) ADMINISTRATIVE      CONTROL

Reduce risk by upgrading training

Introduce work practices that reduce the risk.

This could include limiting the amount of time a person is exposed to a particular hazard.

6) PERSONAL      PROTECTIVE      EQUIPMENT

Reduce risk by upgrading training

When all other control measures are not practicable then appropriate personal protective equipment is needed

To protect workers on a building site from falling objects safety helmets need to be worn.
If you work in a noisy environment the hearing protection is required – eg ear muffs or ear plugs.
For eye protection – eg spectacles/goggles, shields, visors; foot protection – eg shoes with steel cap/safetyboots; for protection from falls - eg harness and fall arrest devices ect..
