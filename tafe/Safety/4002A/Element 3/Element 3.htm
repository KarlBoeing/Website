Recommendation

The aim of the site inspection (Formative Assessment 1) is to identify risks that exists on building site and to make suggestions how these risks can be controlled.

Apart from the site visit a Job Safety Analysis (JSA) (Formative Assessment 2) needs to be prepared to provide a safe working environment for employees, subcontractors and visitors. Appropriate strategies should be introduced to make sure all people working in the area are not subjected to any work-related injuries.

Further recommendations for improving the safety and compliance with the OS&H act and regulations may be required. Recommendations should be provided to address areas of concern.

Consultation process

By law, employers must consult with employees on a range of matters that directly affect (or are likely to directly affect) their health and safety, so far as is reasonably practicable.

Consultation must involve sharing information with employees, giving the employees a reasonable opportunity to express their views and taking those views into account. Under the duty, employers must consult with their employees to enable the employees to contribute to the making of decisions affecting their health, safety and welfare at work.

Consultation must be undertaken by one or a combination of the following means:

a) The establishment of one or more Committees;
b) The election of one or more OS&H Representatives;
c) Other arrangements agreed by the employer and employees .

Employers should give further thought to additional circumstances where consultation could improve OS&H outcomes.

Consider how you can ensure effective & meaningful consultation. One should provide employees with information to enable them to come to an informed view. Decide on the most appropriate and effective consultation arrangement.

• To set up an OS&H Committee(s).
• To set up an OS&H Representative(s).
• To set up Other Agreed Arrangements.

Consultation can be done well and therefore assist in the making of better decisions about OS&H or it can be done poorly.

Effectiveness of control procedures

The hierarchy of control involves starting from the top (Elimination) of the list of risk control options and working your way down, with the last option (PPE) being the least desirable.

There is a sequence of options which offer you a number of ways to approach the hazard control process. An examples is shown below. Work your way down the list, and implement the best measure possible for your situation.

Elimination most effective control measure, involves the removal of the risk (by removing it or changing work processes) eg. remove a noisy machine; cease in-house operations of hazardous work; removing the need for stressful actions and movements by redesigning the work area or work flow etc.
Substitution replacing the plant or substance with another with a lower risk; eg. replace hazardous electrics with hydraulics; purchase less hazardous machinery; using non-flammable solvent in place of a flammable one; use chemicals in pellet or paste form instead of dusty powders etc.
Isolation removing or separating people from the source of the hazard eg. install guards, install roll-over protection on mobile powered plant; install screens or barriers or marking off hazardous areas;
Engineering Controls changing the physical characteristics of plant or workplace to remove or reduce the risk eg. redesign the task, to enable it to be carried out in a different way; modification of tools and equipment; using enclosures eg. enclosing a noisy piece of equipment; guarding machinery; installing local exhaust ventilation; automating a process etc.
Administrative Controls using policies, procedures, signs, training etc. to control risk eg. set up entry permits to operate work systems; install warning signs or danger tags; reducing the number of employees exposed to the hazard; reducing the period of exposure; rotating jobs etc.
Personal Protective Equipment *(PPE) equipment or clothing designed to provide protection eg. safety belts and harnesses, fall-arrest systems; industrial safety gloves and footwear etc.
*PPE should be used as part of an integrated organisational approach to health and safety management. It should complement other control methods, not replace them.

Purpose of Control Measures

In the context of major hazards the purpose of the risk control measures is to eliminate or reduce risk so far as is reasonably practicable. This is achieved, if practicable, by:

  • elimination of hazards;
  • prevention of hazards leading to major incidents;
  • reduction of the magnitude of any major incidents that may occur; and
  • mitigation of the severity of consequences of those major incidents.

    • Risk control measures must be adopted that eliminate or reduce risks to health, safety and property so far as is reasonably practicable.

  • Risk control measures must be adopted for each potential major incident
  • Risk control measures must be adopted for each major incident hazard
  • The adopted risk control measures must take into account the likelihood of the hazard or risk concerned eventuating
  • The adopted risk control measures must take into account the degree of harm that would result if the hazard or risk eventuated.
  • The adopted risk control measures must take into account what the person concerned knows, or ought reasonably to know, about the hazard or risk and any ways of eliminating or reducing the hazard or risk
    • .
  • The adopted control measures must take into account the suitability and availability of risk control measures
  • The adopted control measures must take into account the cost of the risk control measures
  • Are any known deficiencies in risk control measures being progressively addressed?
  • Safety & reliability must be incorporated in design, construction, operation and maintenance of the facility.
  • Risk control measures should be at least consistent with industry practice, and with codes and standards.


Monitor and Review

Once hazards have been identified and controls put in place a process of evaluation needs to be implemented to assess the on-going effectiveness of the control measure and to determine if the control measures introduced any new hazards. This is done by evaluating the risk after a specified period of time.

A risk management program is ongoing. The process only begins when current workplace hazards have been identified and does not end when they are successfully controlled. A system of monitoring and review must be implemented as a method of checking that the controls put in place are achieving the desired outcome.

Building and construction workplaces are a fluid environment with constant changes which must be reflected in the risk assessments. The opportunity for new hazards to occur in the workplace always exists because of new technology, equipment or products, work practices or procedures, or a change in the work environment including new management and staff.

For risk management to be an effective program, hazards associated with changes need to be identified, assessed and eliminated or controlled.


Contingency and Emergency Plans

In an emergency, a fast and effective response is critical. A person's ability to face and handle the situation will depend largely on the amount of training undertaken.

Procedures and specific training for dealing with emergencies will differ in each workplace. So every worker must become familiar with the plans and procedures developed and practise them in their workplace.


JOB SAFETY ANALYSIS (JSA)

A Job Safety Analysis (JSA) is a process of planning for work with safety an integral factor of the way the job is done. It is a method of ensuring that sufficiently skilled manpower, plant/equipment and materials/resources are allocated for a task and all persons ultimately involved are aware of and follow a safe system of work.
