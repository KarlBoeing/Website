Hazard identification is the process used to identify all the possible situations in the workplace where people may be exposed to injury, illness or disease. Inspections are a good way to identify hazards and issues specific to your work environment and activities. Regular workplace inspections must be performed to ensure that a safe working environment is maintained throughout the organisation. These inspections aim to identify any systematically occurring hazards in the workplace and to ensure that safety practices are maintained.

You can either develop your own inspection checklists covering all work areas and tasks carried out in your business, or you can use a checklists and Hazards, Risks and Controls forms.
Some items you may include in your inspections are:

  • Floors & Rooms - floor surfaces condition, torn carpet, uneven surfaces etc., aisles width, exposed cables, Stairs (foot space, slipping treads, adequate handrails etc..
  • Amenities - supply of drinking water, suitable washrooms, hygienic toilets/urina etc.l.
  • Fire and emergency safety - access and exits, firefighting equipment, alarm systems (exits identified, exit doors and equipment unobstructed, evacuation plan in place, fire extinguishers, emergency lighting etc.).
  • Ventilation - natural air flow and air extraction, mechanical (include air conditioners, fans etc), filters clean/inspected, room temperature controll etc..
  • Lightings - sufficient lighting for the tasks, control incoming natural light, artificial lighting causing reflection etc..
  • Electrical - condition and location of cables, plugs, sockets and switches etc..
  • Ergonomic and manual handling - the design of work stations, height of bench tops and desks, seating, tasks involving lifting, carrying, reaching, stretching and repetitionetc..
Hazards can change from day to day, so carry out inspections regularly.

Organisations require that hazards are notified in a manner which enables them to be efficiently and effectively actioned. The notification procedure reflects the need to protect the health and safety of staff, workers and visitors. It ensures that hazards are quickly identified and immediately rectified. Managers and/ or Supervisors are responsible for the provision of Hazard Report Forms at each work area. Copies can also be accessed from the OHS web-sites.

When you finish the workplace inspections all serious hazards identified should be reported. However, if an identified hazard can be corrected immediately, eg moving an extension lead, then that does not require reporting.

Please complete the Hazard Report Form and submit it with your Inspection Report. You can download a form as a word document or as a pdf-document or design your own form..
Duties of safety officers and WorkSafe inspectors

Safety Officer: He or she may be appointed by a builder and is responsible for monitoring and assessing safety hazards or unsafe situations, and for developing measures for ensuring personnel safety.
Workplace Health and Safety Officers provide expert advice to employers to help them meet their obligations under the OS&H-Act. They are trained to identify health and safety hazards and help implement risk assessments in the workplace.

WorkSafe Inspectors

WorkSafe inspectors are appointed by the Commissioner and trained to enforce the OS&H-Act, assist with the resolution of issues in workplaces and provide advice to employers and employees to how best to improve workplace safety and health performance. When WorkSafe inspectors visit your workplace they will use a checklist of common hazards to look for key safety elements. If a builder fails to meet safety standards, inspectors will issue the builder with verbal instructions, an improvement notice, a prohibition notice or any combination of these. It's also their duty to investigate employee complaints regarding their workplace, to advise workers and employers on safety in the workplace, and to ensure that safety and health rules are adhered to.
