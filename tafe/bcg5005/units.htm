Symbols, units and formula (Revision)

Unit names (symbols) are standard prefixes that stand for some of the powers of 10 such as 0.001 (milli), 1000 (kilo). For instance, since the prefix kilo (k) stands for 1000, 1 kilometre (km) equals 1000 metres (m), and any change from metres to kilometres or vice versa simply involves a decimal point or zeros as shown below.

Some Prefixes and Units
Prefix & Symbol Meaning Value Factor
milli (m) one thousandth 0.001 10-3
kilo (k) a thousand 1000 103
mega (M) a million 1,000,000 106
giga (G) a thousand million 1,000,000,000 109

AREA SI unit: square metre (m²)
1000 000 square millimetres (mm²) = 1 square metre (m²)
10 000 square metres (m²) = 1 hectare (ha)
100 hectares (ha) = 1 square kilometre (km²)

Students need to familiarise themselves with these quantities, prefixes and units. If you learn them by heart it'll be easier to perform any calculation (volumes; bearing stresses, forces or areas etc.)

Most people do not make a distinction between mass and weight. The mass is always a constant figure here on Earth but the weight can change depending where we are. If we would build on the moon the mass of concrete (2500 kg/m3) would be the same as on Earth but the weight will be much lighter.

As we use 10 m/s² as gravitational accelerating the conversion is quite easy :
1 kg = 10 N; 100 kg = 1000 N or 1 kN (Remember you use a decimal system for conversion)

Weight of concrete m3 of concrete (W = m × g) 2500 kg/m3 × ~10 m/s² = 25 000 N or 25 kN

However, the acceleration due to gravity on the moon is 1.62 m/s². This is approximately 1/6 that of the acceleration due to gravity on Earth, 9.81 m/s². Therefore the weight of 1 kg is only 1.62 N and that of a m3 of concrete only 4.05 kN (2500 × 1.62 = 4050N) compared with 25 kN on Earth.

Example 1:
Calculate the Weight of a squared pad footing (1.25² × 0.50 m)
Solution:
From above the weight of 1 m3 concrete is 25 kN. The volume of concrete is
