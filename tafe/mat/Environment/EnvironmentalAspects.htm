Environmental Aspects

Measuring the environmental impact of Buildings and Building Materials.

The emphasis on minimising environmental impacts of building and building materials has led to the development of several types of environmental assessment and management tools.

These vary considerably in their type and intent and the complexity of their methodology. Some are used to access individual products or construction elements, such as air conditioners or timber chairs. Others are used to model the performance and effects of whole buildings or groups of buildings. They are then used to limit or manage their impacts. Governments have funded the development of some of these tools while others are prepared and promoted by environmental certification companies.

Most of the tools have several major aspects in common. They generally seek to measure and rate:

  • the energy cost and amounts of pollutants (solid and liquid waste, greenhouse gases, toxins and particles) resulting from extraction, manufacture, and transport.,
  • the environmental and energy cost of fabrication and on-site waste and packaging
  • the environmental impact occurring during the building's life from required operational energy, transport and generated pollutants.
  • the environmental impact at the end of the life of the building or component (for example, the cost or implications of disposal).

Not all of the tools are routinely used as the assessments are complex, lengthy and require detailed information and reporting. Also, as they differ in methodology, the results from different tools cannot be compared directly and may differ considerably.

The types of methods or systems listed below include the recently developed National Australian Building Environmental Rating System and overviews of environmental management systems, environmental impact assessment, environmental audits, eco labelling, and the Building Material Ecological Sustainability Index.

Assessing the Environmental Impact of Building Materials

A life cycle assessment is one method that is used to measure the environmental impacts of building products. Alternative terms for a life cycle assessment include cradle to grave analysis, eco-balance, product lifecycle analysis, and resource and environmental profile analysis.

The aim of a life cycle assessment is to identify, quantify and assess the impact of the energy and materials used and wastes released to the environment throughout the life of a building product.

There are many life cycle assessment methodologies and the models vary in their complexity. They range from full life cycle assessments (used for publications, policy decisions and marketing claims) to streamlined approaches (used for comparisons and usually with the aid of a software tool) to guidelines and checklists.

Typically, there will be several stages that are considered. These include:

  • mining/extraction/harvesting and transport to place of manufacture;
  • manufacturing;
  • on site construction including transport to the building site;
  • occupancy and maintenance;
  • demolition and disposal and recycling.

At each of these stages, there are a number of variables that can alter the outcome of the assessment. For example, the length of the life of the building may vary considerably depending on the durability of the building materials, fashion, demographic change or other factors such as recycling and deconstruction. So, even if a building has a relatively short life span, its impact may effectively be offset to some degree by recycling of the materials.

By allowing for comparison between various building products, a life cycle assessment can assist in making an informed decision about the choice of building materials. Because this type of assessment can also be used as a marketing tool to support the use of one material over another, it is important to view the environmental assessment critically and consider other factors such as structural, economic and aesthetic performance.

There are a number of methods available to conduct a life cycle assessment. This makes meaningful comparisons difficult. International organisations such as the International Standards Organisation and the Society of Environmental Toxicology and Chemistry are working towards standardising the process.

Life cycle assessments take time to carry out and require detailed information. Access to life cycle information on a range of building products is not yet readily available. Market demand for this information together with further development and refinement of the assessment tools should improve the availability of information.

The energy consumed to manufacture building products

There is an increasing awareness of the need to conserve scarce resources by using products and practices that are energy efficient.

Much of the energy consumed in every day living is derived from fossil fuels and this use can have significant environmental impacts by increasing greenhouse emissions.

Until recently, efforts to improve the energy efficiency of buildings targeted their operational energy. That is, the energy used in the operation of the building (heat, power, and light). As a result, strategies focused on improving the performance of existing buildings and developing better practice for the design of new buildings.

More recently, emphasis has been placed on the energy invested in the materials used to construct and maintain the building over its lifetime, that is, their embodied energy. This is also referred to as life cycle energy consumption.

Recent studies have shown that over its lifespan, the embodied energy of a building can be greater than the operating energy. To obtain the best long term result, building design must consider both embodied energy and operational energy.

The stages considered in an assessment of the energy consumed over the life of a building or building material are similar to the stages of a life cycle analysis and include the energy required to:

  • mine/extract/harvest the raw material and transport to the processing centre;
  • manufacture the building materials;
  • transport to the construction site;
  • maintenance and refurbishment of the building; and
  • demolish recycle and finally dispose of waste material.

The consumption of energy during each of these stages can have similar environmental impacts to the consumption of energy in the operation of the building.

There are two major methods for reporting and using embodied energy.

  1. Gross Energy Requirement (GER). This includes all of the energy inputs for a building in all stages listed above. This can be a very complex process as there are many variables within each stage. For example, the energy input into the construction of the building also includes the transport of the workers, equipment and materials.
  2. Process Energy Requirement (PER). The PER is the component of the GER that relates directly to the manufacture of the building material. The PER makes an allowance for procurement of raw materials, transport between manufacturing processes and the actual manufacture of the building material. This method more readily allows for comparisons to be made between various building materials.

However, it is important that embodied energy comparisons are not viewed in isolation. For example, the insulating properties of a high embodied energy material may save significantly on a building’s heating energy requirements.
